# Exploring The Movie Landscape

Going into this, my expectations are very low; I doubt I'll find a satisfying answer. When it comes to big production movies, licensing is the name of the game. I mean, this is an industry that put all those warnings and labels on DVDs a couple decades ago when people (myself included) were just renting movies; they might as well have used the [IT Crowd version](https://www.quotes.net/show-quote/41023) of the script. So anyway, the scope here is to find out if those mainstream films are available anywhere as a digital download for purchase.

## The One Exception

I have had the pleasure to legally download exactly one movie up to this point. That nugget is [The Man from Earth: Holocene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Man_from_Earth:_Holocene). I really enjoyed the first movie and when I heard there was a second one I went to look it up where I could stream it. To my surprise, it was made available for download by the creators themselves as a torrent!

To be fair, this was likely part of a social experiment on alternative revenue generation, download the movie first, donate some money afterward if you so wish. If I'm not mistaken, Radiohead did that a few years prior and other artists have occasionally done the same. Nevertheless, this is the closest to a digital copy of a movie that can be downloaded and stored with the permission of the publisher. It doesn't make it DRM-free per se, but it is the only such instance I know of.

## Searching Online

Usually the joke goes "I had to get all the way to the second page of Google before I could find the answer". Page after page, however, all I could find on either Google or DuckDuckGo, are the same few articles or Reddit threads from several years ago with the occasional recent inquiry "are we there yet?". Nope, far from it.

These are the ones:

* [LifeHacker](https://lifehacker.com/can-i-get-drm-free-movies-and-tv-shows-without-pirating-477555875)
* [Reddit](https://www.reddit.com/r/selfhosted/comments/1djaf2b/buying_drmfree_movies_and_shows/)

There is also an old forums thread from GOG from which I gather they tried to sell DRM-free movies at some point. Nowadays, the URL itself simply redirects to their games page, so I'm guessing it didn't go well.

Now, there is a well respected website - [Defective By Design](https://www.defectivebydesign.org/guide/video) which actually got me introduced to the topic many years ago, and it does list some sources for DRM-free videos. From those, the two I can actually check for myself are:

* [Internet Archive](https://archive.org/) | Terabytes upon terabytes of material available to stream or download. This is an important repository for preservation; it contains so many classics like Charlie Chaplin movies and anything that belongs in the public domain from what I understand. It also hosts so many other things though, like this awesome [1992 Godfather Theme Cover by Slash](https://archive.org/details/SLASH_The_Godfather_Theme_1992). Not sure where this falls. I just enjoy the performance. I have not watched this video since the early 2000s. Slash is eternal.
* [Vimeo On Demand](https://vimeo.com/ondemand) | The majority of content is short films, some are available to purchase and stream but many are available for purchase and download. This is a genuinely good resource with a niche audience in mind. The only offering I could recognize was this [2014 Marc Maron special](https://vimeo.com/ondemand/21794) but hey, something!

There are a couple of other links listed, one in French, one about Science Theater 3000, but I'm not keen to explore those further. Maybe suffice to say that as far as a performance on video goes, not everything belongs to Hollywood or big studios. So checking at the studio's website, or the artist's website there is a slim chance to outright purchase a digital copy.

## Other Avenues

Despite the mild interest in the digital ownership of movies, life continues, so a practical solution is in order. From the online forums and my own observation, the following seems to be what people end up doing.

### Just Stream It

It works for many people but comes with tradeoffs, besides not owning anything. Fewer subscriptions, more limited catalog, but also cheaper. More subscriptions, more to watch, but pricey. With many services prohibiting account sharing, the costs add up fast. One interesting idea is active account management, pause one service, subscribe to another one, come back to the first and so on. Since the contracts are typically month-based, this is feasible and allows access to broader catalog. 

### VPN & Eye Patch

There are at least a few videos on YouTube examining how this current generation (Gen Z) is returning increasingly to piracy. Can you blame them? Job market is not great for new graduates, meanwhile, streaming is not as rewarding as it used to be a few years back. In the end it comes down to having options and making choices yourself as opposed to being dragged into a certain platform's recommendations. It is a fascinating topic in itself, but a gray zone legally speaking.

### Digital Purchases

Both Apple TV and Amazon Video allow purchasing digital copies of movies. I'm not sure about YouTube TV but I would assume yes. There's also other providers like Vudu and more. Is it a good option? In many ways yes. You don't have to worry about local hosting, backups and any technical details which become increasingly relevant as you move up in picture quality. From a price standpoint, every month there are different sales, so that also checks. The downside of course is you are locked into an ecosystem for as long as you want access to those movies. There are also many cases of digital content being erased without justification which is scary.

### Buy and Rip Physical Copies

Generally accepted as the best solution from both a legal and ethical standpoint, but it is involved. The idea is that you buy movies and shows in DVD or Blue Ray disc, wherever you find them, then use your computer to digitize them and access them locally, or perhaps over the home network using something like Plex or Jellyfin. The argument is that you paid for the physical copy and you're just making a digital backup. After the initial investment in hardware and setup, this sounds like a solid solution. It is not for everyone, and it definitely is not cheap but you get to have a physical and digital collection of movies and shows.

### My Own Solution

I have thought about this a few times over the years and what I have decided is a combination of streaming and digital purchasing. My reasoning is that I have an iCloud+ subscription which greatly benefits my family, so I will likely keep it for a long time and I get TV+ in that bundle. I also have an Amazon Prime account which comes with Prime Video, so that is also available. Between the two services I can watch some good shows that I don't necessarily need to own, and also a ton of UFO films (looking at you Prime) that I certainly have no need to own, though they are entertaining. For everything else, Tubi is a great platform in the US. It is ad-supported but I don't mind. Since I don't pay, I don't have to worry if I'm making the most out of it. I actually watched the entire Mr Robot series there last year because it was in-between moving platforms and not available elsewhere. 

On the other hand, there are movies that I keep rewatching every few years, like the Matrix or Lord of The Rings, so it makes sense I purchase those when they are on sale, and just rewatch them from my own library. I do this from the Apple TV app. For running TV shows, there is an option to subscribe to the current season too. Instead of paying a recurring streaming subscription to watch just one show, it is cheaper to just buy the season pass for that show. 

I fully realize this is not a global solution, but it is a decent one for US residents. A couple hundred bucks go a long way to building a library of favorite movies and shows. Then from there on just a purchase here and there. Nothing crazy. Probably much cheaper actually over the span of 2-3 years compared to multiple streaming services. Pricing aside, it has given me back some control and the ability to choose what I watch. I don't worry about watching everything TV+ or Prime Video has to offer because I am paying for their parent subscriptions anyway. But paying $25/month for Netflix and another $35/month for the Hulu/Disney/HBO combo certainly does create that guilt.

## In Conclusion

Well, as far as DRM-free goes, no luck as expected with big studios; and it seems like the situation will remain like this for the foreseeable future. For short films, Vimeo On Demand has some options which is great if you enjoy those. For everyone else, we are stuck with a combination of streaming, buying from the big guys or finding an alternative path.
