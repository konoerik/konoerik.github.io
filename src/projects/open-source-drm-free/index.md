# Exploring Open Source & DRM-Free

The idea here is simple. Without giving up my current ecosystem (mainly Apple devices and a few subscription services), spend some time using open source software for my daily computing needs and consuming DRM-free digital media for a change. Not in a strict manner, not with an absolutist mindset, not with a specific end goal in mind. Just exploring what the current landscape looks like.

## Motivation, Expectations and Setup

The main motivation is to explore the ideas of digital media consumption and ownership in a free and open source environment as an alternative or a complementary solution to proprietary software and licensed material. This is a theme I have explored once before, over a decade ago. Now I'm coming back to it after having been on the other side (Apple Music, Amazon Prime, Netflix and so on).

My expectations are realistic: I will not be giving up my iPhone anytime soon and I will not be cancelling all my subscriptions (although I did make a significant dent in that direction recently as a separate effort). I just want some time away from the constant advertisement and features being pushed downstream. I want to play music I own, read some of the billions (exaggeration) of Humble Bundle books I have accumulated over the years and write about it along the way.

Maybe I am just looking for some reason to get back to Linux. That part is also true. I never left Linux really, professionally speaking, but I have not seriously used it on a personal computer in over a decade, so I'm curious to see what it feels like as a daily driver again.

The irony is I will be using a 2018 Mac Mini for this. With Apple's transition to Apple Silicon, this little guy got left behind a couple years into the M-series; not compatible with more recent macOS, and no security updates for the last supported one. It is quite the performer however and perfect for this experiment: Intel i7 6-core (12 threads), 16GB of DDR4, 1TB NVMe SSD; almost entirely Linux-compatible out of the box, with the exception of the Broadcom WiFi/BT chip, for which the folks at [t2linux](https://wiki.t2linux.org/) have kindly provided a solution. Did I mention it has an expansion base with extra ports and SD slots? This thing is a great desktop PC with a small footprint; it begs to be put to good use again.

## Areas of Exploration

**OS & Apps** | So many options! This has always been exciting when looking into Linux distros; it can become a time sink quickly also; nevertheless happy to jump back into it and report the results.

**Music** | This is probably the easiest to fill in; Bandcamp is the name, and I'm finding out there are other high quality options also since I last checked; heck even Apple and Amazon allow purchasing DRM free albums (although at lower quality).

**Video** | Big studio movies are only available in theaters and streaming services; maybe there are other platforms out there for independent films or international cinema that allow straight up purchase and download.

**Books** | Most books found in physical copies are DRM protected in their digital form; there are tons of books on Project Gutenberg / Standard Ebooks however and there are license-free publishers out there. Perhaps an opportunity to revisit.

**Comics** | Same story as books - big names are only sold through licensed stores (Kindle, Apple Books etc.) but there are other/newer publishers out there that allow traditional purchasing. And don't forget Humble Bundle.

**Games** | This one could potentially be tough on two fronts: no DRM and Linux compatible. I mostly play indie games however, so I think the odds are with me for finding decent options. As far as the Linux portion goes, there has been tremendous adoption in the last few years, both native and via translation layers such as Wine/Proton.
